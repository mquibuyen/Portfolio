Talking Points



Mobile Design // iOS 8 // Prototyping // Marvel // Illustrator // Photoshop // Web Development



As a Capitol Hill staffer I have seen tons of correspondence and emails sent to our office from constituents on various opinions or political statements. However, the problem is that sometimes our content management system (CMS) does not aggregate the details correctly or the data you're trying to display will be presented poorly and can create confusion among the legislative staff members. Another issue is that the so-called modern CMS we use for government work in my opinion has a lot of features but is lacking in good user flows to quickly and accurately reach the result you're looking for. This Bloc Apprenticeship project had me tasked with coming up with an idea for a mobile app to help people easily gain consensus from a community quickly and efficiently. Also, use the polling app as an added solution in our toolbox in conjunction with our CMS in order for government staffers to paint a better picture of both sides of the issue to their superiors.

Goals

  • 1. Simplify polling to easily gauge consensus

  • 2. Create a method to reduce spam or flaming of other users

  • 3. Ensure the data gathering is unique and secure

Research

I started looking at other mobile apps that had a polling or political background such as Capitol Bells, Brigade, Cloakroom, and Whip Watch. I also looked at different content management systems (CMS) that helped people get engaged in the political process with web apps like Popvox, Countable, and Quorum. As my mentor heard my ideas regarding the app features, we started to break down whether or not having all the features of a CMS were necessary to build our app. When we decided on details for the functions we drew inspiration Instagram as a starting point because it does one thing really well and that's taking pictures, why not do the same for polling? A survey was created to see what polling applications people like to use and the data showed us that a vast majority of users prefer Google Forms as the platform to find consensus in various topics. The data was helpful in creating the initial wireframes and functions that would go into the prototype.

User Flow Wireframing

Wireframes were created based on the research data received and the flow was inspired by the various polling apps out on the market. We decided to test these wireframes and test the user flow on Usability Hub. The first Nav Flow results showed us that more than half of the testers were able to complete the process of answering a poll, then viewing the comments, and finally post a comment. However, the second Nav Flow testers were not as successful, where only 22% were able to complete the process of logging to the app, selecting a location, and creating a poll. I believe the reason the first test was easier to do is because it was a shorter flow and followed similar patterns in apps like Facebook. The second test showed me that the app needed more attention focused on the poll creation process because the users could not identify the plus sign as adding a poll but would rather click the "new" button which was supposed to be a sorting mechanism.

Branding and Prototyping

I created a logo that exemplified having your voice heard and chose a lighter political color scheme, which was a combination of reds, whites, and blues. Then, I created high-fidelity screens using Photoshop and the Teehanlax iOS 8 templates as well as the Apple iOS guidelines online to ensure consistent spacing or patterns that would be easily recognized by the users. Once this process was complete, I put together a prototype by using Marvel to see how the mobile app would work as if it was a live product.



Test and Iterate

I tested the Talking Points app user flow on Usability Hub again to see if there was any improvement and the Nav Flow test results had an increase on conversion rates for creating and commenting on polls. After my mentor reviewed the initial prototype we had to make a few changes regarding posting comments and how the test results would show up on the polls because they were not as aesthetically pleasing as a finished mobile app. I took all of those critiques into account as well as created a prototype marketing landing page and what you will see on Marvel is the final mobile app prototype.
